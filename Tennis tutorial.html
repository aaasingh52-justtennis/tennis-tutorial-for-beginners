Every Point Starts with a Serve

A tennis match is made up of points — the smallest unit of scoring. One player wins each point, and the other loses it.

Every point begins with a shot called the serve. One player hits the ball to their opponent to start the action.

The server stands behind their baseline and hits the ball diagonally across the court to their opponent. The serve is the only shot in tennis where you initiate the action — every other shot you are reacting to an incoming ball from your opponent.

Key Point: The serve must land in a specific area called the service box. If it doesn't, it's called a "fault."

Don't worry if terms like "baseline" and "service box" are unfamiliar — we'll explain them all in detail on the next page.

Let's look at the court and see where the players stand. Player A (red) is the server. Player B (blue) is the receiver. Notice they stand diagonally opposite each other. Throughout the serve and rally sections of this tutorial, Player A will always be the server and Player B the receiver — so whenever you see the court, you'll know who's who.

What is a Fault?

If the serve doesn't land in the service box, it's called a fault.

The service box is a rectangle, so it has 4 sides. If you're trying to make the ball bounce inside it, you can miss on any of those 4 sides:

Try each button to see the different ways a serve can be a fault. Notice that in each case, the ball misses one of the 4 boundaries of the service box. (Of course, it's possible to miss two boundaries at once — for example, a ball could be both long AND wide — but these demos illustrate missing just one at a time.)

Foot Fault: There's also the foot fault! The server's feet can't touch on or over the baseline before they hit the ball. If they do, it's called a foot fault - and it counts the same as any other fault. (You'll often see players jump and land inside the court — that's fine, as long as their feet left the ground from behind the line and they landed after hitting the ball!)
🤔 Why did they catch the ball?
Sometimes on TV, you'll see a player throw the ball up and then just catch it or let it drop.

This is allowed! If the server doesn't like their toss (maybe the wind moved it), they can catch it or let it drop and try again without penalty — as many times as they like. However, once they swing at the ball, the serve counts: if they miss the ball entirely (an "air swing"), that's actually a fault!

So to recap, a fault is called when: the ball doesn't land in the correct service box (too long, too wide, wrong side, or into the net), or the server commits a foot fault, or the server swings and misses the ball. Those are the only things — catching or dropping the toss is never a fault.

First Serve and Second Serve

Here's where tennis gives you a second chance! If your first serve is a fault, you get to try again on the same point — nobody has won or lost yet. This second attempt is called your second serve.

The Rule: You get TWO attempts to hit a good serve on each point.
  • First serve: Your first attempt
  • Second serve: Your second attempt (only if the first was a fault)

If your first serve goes in, great! Play continues and you don't need the second serve.

If your first serve is a fault, no problem — take your second serve. The point is still up for grabs.

The Double Fault

But what if BOTH serves are faults? This is called a double fault, and it's bad news for the server.

Double Fault = You Lose the Point!
If you fault on both your first serve AND your second serve, your opponent wins the point automatically.

This is why the second serve is so important - you really don't want to miss it!

The Let Serve

There's one special situation we need to cover: what happens if the ball clips the net but still lands in the correct service box?

This is called a let, and the serve is simply replayed. It doesn't count as a fault, and it doesn't count as a good serve either - you just try again.

Let Serve Rules:
  • Ball clips the net AND lands in the correct service box = Let (replay)
  • Ball clips the net AND lands outside the service box = Fault (no replay)
  • Ball hits the net AND doesn't go over = Fault (we covered this one already!)

A let can happen on either your first or second serve. If it's a let, you simply retake that same serve — you don't lose an attempt. One important detail: a let on the second serve doesn't give you your first serve back. If your first serve was a fault and your second serve is a let, you retake the second serve — the first-serve fault still stands.

Watch the animation carefully: when the ball clips the net, you'll see the trajectory line change angle and the ball slow down slightly — just like in real life when the net deflects and absorbs some energy from the ball. You'll also hear a beep the moment the ball touches the net, just like the real net sensor!

📺 TV Insight: On television, it's often hard to see the ball clip the net. If you hear a loud BEEP sound or see the umpire interrupt play instantly, a sensor on the net detected the vibration of a "Let."

Compare all three: Let = clips net, lands IN (replay). Net+Out = clips net, lands OUT (fault). Net Fault = hits net and stops (fault).

Why Take Risks on the First Serve?

Now you understand why professional players often hit their first serve much harder and with more risk than their second serve!

On the first serve:

  • You can afford to take risks because you have a backup
  • A fast, aggressive serve (hit with pace and aimed at difficult spots) is harder for your opponent to return
  • If you miss, no problem - you still have your second serve

On the second serve:

  • You MUST get this in or you lose the point
  • Players usually hit slower and safer
  • This gives the opponent a better chance to attack
Strategy: This is why "first serve percentage" is an important statistic you'll see on TV. It simply means: of all your first serves, what percentage landed in?

In professional tennis, around 60% is typically where the best players land. But why not higher? The answer is about the trade-off between serve effectiveness and safety (getting the ball in).

If a player could hit huge, powerful serves AND get 80% of them in, that would be fantastic — more power to them! But in practice, that almost never happens. The harder and more aggressive you serve, usually the fewer go in. So when you see a very high first serve percentage (say, 80%+), it usually means the player is serving too safely — and therefore their first serve is likely not much harder to return than their second serve. This is especially common in recreational tennis.

Conversely, if a player's percentage drops too low (say, 40%), they're usually going for too much power and therefore having to fall back on their weaker second serve more often than they would like, giving their opponent lots of easier balls to attack.

The Numbers: Why ~60% Is the Sweet Spot

You might be wondering: why not just blast every first serve as hard as possible? Or play it safe and get every first serve in? Let's do the maths.

There's a key trade-off in tennis:

The Trade-Off: The more aggressive your first serve (faster, closer to the lines), the harder it is to get in — but the more likely you are to win the point when it does go in. A safe, slow serve is easy to land, but also easy for your opponent to attack.

Let's imagine three players, each serving 20 points. They all have the same second serve (safe, and they win about half of points when forced to use it). To keep things simple, we'll assume the second serve always goes in — no double faults. The only difference is how aggressive their first serve is.

Wins on second serve: 2 out of 4 (50%)

😎 Player 3: "Smart balance" — 60% first serve in

Wins on second serve: 4 out of 8 (50%)

What happened?

Player 1 had the most lethal first serve — winning 83% of points when it landed. But it only landed 30% of the time, so they were constantly falling back on their weaker second serve, giving their opponent lots of easy chances.

Player 2 got almost every first serve in — but because the serve was so safe and slow, their opponent attacked it easily. A 56% win rate on first serve is barely better than the second serve, so all those first serves landing (80%) didn't help much.

Player 3 found the sweet spot. Their first serve was aggressive enough to win 75% of points when it landed — not as lethal as Player 1's, but it went in twice as often (60% instead of 30%). That balance gave them the most total wins.

The bottom line: Getting your first serve in is valuable because you win more points on first serve than second serve. But a first serve that's too easy to return is almost as bad as missing it entirely. The ~60% sweet spot balances these two forces.

The Rally

You've completed the serve section — you now understand how every point in tennis begins. Next, let's see what happens after the serve lands in.

So the serve has landed in the service box — what happens next? The receiver hits the ball back, and the rally begins!

A rally is a back-and-forth exchange of shots. Players take turns hitting the ball over the net and into the opponent’s side of the court. Each player must hit the ball back before it bounces a second time on their side (it can be hit after one bounce or hit straight from the air before it bounces, but cannot bounce twice) — if it bounces twice, they lose the point.

Key Change — Where Can the Ball Land?
During the serve, the ball had to land in the small service box. During a rally, the ball can land anywhere on your opponent’s side of the court — the entire area between the baseline, sidelines, and net. That’s a much bigger target!

📺 TV Terminology: Shot Direction
You'll hear commentators describe shots using three main directions:
Cross-court — hitting diagonally, from one corner toward the opposite corner.
Down the line — hitting straight ahead, close to and parallel to the nearest sideline.
Down the middle — hitting toward the centre of the opponent's court, away from both sidelines.
⚠️ Don't mix these up with serve directions!
Serves use a completely different set of terms: down the T, out wide, and at the body. Those describe where the ball lands in the small service box.

Rally directions — cross-court, down the line, and down the middle — describe where a shot goes across the full court during a rally.

The two sets never overlap: you won't hear a commentator say a serve went "cross-court" or that a rally shot went "down the T." If you hear "down the T," it's always about a serve. If you hear "cross-court," it's always about a rally shot.

Now watch the animation to see a serve followed by a short rally. Notice how the green zone switches sides after each shot:

Both players stay behind their baselines, moving side to side to reach the ball. The serve lands in the service box, the return comes back cross-court, and then the next shot goes down the line. The rally continues until one player fails to return the ball — they might hit it into the net, hit it past the boundaries of the court, or let the ball bounce twice on their side before making contact. We’ll look at all the ways a rally can end in detail in a later section.

🤔 What about the net?
Remember how the ball clipping the net during a serve is a “let” and gets replayed? During a rally, there’s no such thing — if the ball clips the net and lands on your opponent’s side, play continues as normal. No replay!

The Three Phases of a Point

Now that you’ve seen a rally in action, let’s break down how every point is structured. The first few shots each have different rules and characteristics:

1. The Serve
Must land in the small service box. The server is in full control: they choose when to hit, how fast, and where to aim.
2. The Return
The receiver must let the serve bounce before hitting it back. The serve is usually the fastest shot in a point — which is one reason the rules require it to land in the much smaller service box rather than anywhere on the opponent’s side. Because the serve must land in the diagonal service box, the receiver positions themselves on that side of the court, ready to react to the full range of serve placements — from down the T to out wide. Even so, because of the speed, the return is often more about getting the ball back in play than hitting an aggressive shot — unless the serve is weak. The return can land anywhere on the opponent’s side.
3. Rally shots (after the return)
Now it’s fully open play. Players can hit the ball either out of the air or after one bounce, and it can land anywhere on the opponent’s side. The server’s first shot after getting the ball back is sometimes called the “serve +1” or the “first rally shot” — it’s the first shot for either player that’s completely unrestricted (since even the return must be played after a bounce).
Summary
Serve — Restricted: must land in the small service box.
Return — Restricted: must let the ball bounce first before hitting it back.
Rally shots — As unrestricted as it gets in tennis: hit it out of the air or after a bounce, to anywhere on the opponent's side.

Groundstrokes: Forehand and Backhand

During a rally, the most common shots are groundstrokes — shots where the ball bounces once on your side and then you hit it back before it bounces a second time. Almost every rally consists mostly of groundstrokes hit from near the baseline.

There are two types, based on which side of your body you hit the ball:

Forehand
A shot hit on the same side as your dominant hand. If you’re right-handed, that’s a ball coming to your right side. The forehand is almost always hit with one hand on the racket.
Backhand
A shot hit on the opposite side from your dominant hand. If you’re right-handed, that’s a ball coming to your left side. Players hit the backhand either one-handed or two-handed — you’ll see both styles on TV.

When you hear commentators say “beautiful forehand” or “solid backhand,” they’re describing which side of the body the player hit the ball from.

Why the Forehand Is Easier

The forehand is generally the easier and more powerful shot, and most players prefer hitting forehands whenever they can. So what makes it easier? The answer is in how your body moves.

Look at the forehand sequence (top). The player’s hitting shoulder rotates from back to front — it’s the same natural motion your body uses when you throw a ball. Your whole body uncoils into the shot, generating power effortlessly.

Now look at the backhand (bottom). The arm has to swing across the body to reach the ball. The hitting shoulder stays more forward instead of rotating freely. It’s a less natural movement, which is why it’s harder to generate the same power and consistency.

The key difference: On a forehand, your body opens up naturally into the shot (like throwing). On a backhand, your body has to work across itself — fighting its own structure rather than using it.
🤔 Why do opponents target the backhand?
Now you know! Because the backhand is a less natural movement for most players, it tends to be their weaker side. Hitting to your opponent’s backhand is one of the oldest and simplest strategies in tennis. Watch for it on TV — you’ll see players deliberately aim for their opponent’s backhand over and over.
🤔 The other side of the coin: “running around” the backhand
Just as opponents target the backhand, players themselves will try to avoid hitting one whenever they can. If a slow or high ball comes to their backhand side and they have enough time, many players will move extra steps around the ball so they can hit it as a forehand instead. This is called “running around the backhand” — and you’ll hear commentators point it out on TV. It costs the player time and court position, but they’d rather hit their stronger, more natural shot.
📺 Predicting the return: forehand or backhand?
Now that you understand forehand vs backhand, here’s a useful trick for watching tennis on TV. If you know which side the server is serving from (deuce or ad) and where the serve is aimed, you can often predict whether the return will be a forehand or a backhand — before it’s even hit.

Think about a right-handed receiver:
• Deuce side, down the T → the ball arrives on the receiver’s left → backhand return
• Deuce side, out wide → the ball arrives on the receiver’s right → forehand return
• Ad side, down the T → the ball arrives on the receiver’s right → forehand return
• Ad side, out wide → the ball arrives on the receiver’s left → backhand return

Serves aimed at the body are the exception — the ball comes straight at the receiver, so the return could be either forehand or backhand depending on which way they move.

Try this when watching a match: combine the serve side + serve placement → which side of the receiver’s body the ball arrives on → forehand or backhand. You’ll be surprised how quickly you can start predicting returns!

Why Come Forward?

During a rally, you don't have to wait for the ball to bounce. If you move forward and hit the ball before it bounces, that shot is called a volley. You'll hear commentators say "forehand volley" or "backhand volley" — forehand means the shot was on the player's dominant hand side, backhand means the opposite side, same as with groundstrokes. The only difference is the ball is hit before it bounces.

But why would a player leave the safety of the baseline and move forward? Because being closer to the net gives two big advantages:

The Angle Advantage
The closer you are to the net, the wider the range of angles you can hit. Click each button below and watch how the shaded landing zone grows as Player A moves forward. The number shows the total angle of the cone — pay attention to how dramatically it opens up!

From the baseline (orange, ~22°), the player can reach the far corners, but the landing zone is concentrated deep in the opponent's court — there's no way to hit short, wide angles from that far back.

From the volley position (blue, ~65°), the cone nearly triples! This is actually where most volleys are played from in real tennis — it gives a great balance of angle and reaction time. The player can now hit much wider and shorter on the opponent's side.

From right at the net (green, ~160°), the range is enormous. The player can hit sharp angles to either sideline or drive it deep. Almost the entire opponent's court is within reach.

🤔 Why can't you hit those short wide shots from the baseline?
It's a rule of geometry: the further the ball has to travel to clear the net, the further it will travel after the net before it bounces. From the baseline, that total flight is long. If you try to hit a sharp angle from back there, the ball is traveling sideways for so long that it will fly past the sideline before it bounces. At the net, the distance to the "hurdle" is tiny, so the distance after the net is also tiny — letting you tuck those sharp angles safely inside the court.

These wider angles apply to any shot played further up the court, but they're especially true for volleys — a ball hit out of the air is usually intercepted higher than a ball that's already bounced, especially if you are coming in to volley a weak ball. That higher contact point opens up even more angles.

The Time Advantage
There's a second advantage to being closer to the net: taking time away from your opponent. This works in two ways. First, you intercept the ball sooner — it reaches you at the net in half the time it would take to reach the baseline, giving your opponent less time to recover their position after their shot. Second, your volley travels a shorter distance back to them, so it arrives sooner too. The combined effect is dramatic — better angles for you, and far less time for your opponent to react.

So the advantages are clear — but how does a player actually get to the net?

Getting to the Net

There are two main ways you'll see a player get to the net on TV:

During a Rally: The Approach Shot
An approach shot is the last shot a player hits before coming forward to the net. It's not a different type of stroke — it's just a regular groundstroke, but what makes it an "approach shot" is what happens after: the player moves forward into the volley position, ready to finish the point. The approach shot is usually aimed deep into the opponent's court to keep them pinned back.

There are two common triggers:

1. A short ball forces them forward. A short ball is one that bounces shorter than usual, around the service line area rather than deep near the baseline. If the player stayed at the baseline to hit this short ball, the ball might bounce twice by the time it reached the baseline, or would be on the way down close to its second bounce — which is not a comfortable height to hit from. Such short balls are better played inside the baseline, where the ball is close to its peak height after the bounce. And after stepping forward to hit a short ball, it usually makes more sense to keep coming forward towards the net rather than retreat back behind the baseline, as we saw with the advantages in the previous section.

2. Their own shot creates the opportunity. Sometimes a player hits a great shot from the baseline — one that pushes their opponent wide or deep — and sees their opponent scrambling. That's an invitation to move forward and take control of the net.
Off the Serve: Serve and Volley
Instead of waiting for a short ball or creating an opportunity during a rally, some players rush straight to the net immediately after serving. The serve itself is what sets up the net position — there's no approach shot involved. This is called serve and volley, and it's a classic aggressive style of play. The server is betting that their serve will be strong enough to produce a weak return that they can volley away.
📺 TV Terminology
"Coming to net" — when a player moves forward to the net during a rally, after an approach shot.
"Serve and volley" — when a player rushes the net straight after serving.
"Great approach shot" — when a player hits a strong shot before coming forward — the approach is what sets up the volley.

One important thing to note: a player who comes to net doesn't always need to hit a volley. Sometimes just the act of moving forward puts the opponent under so much pressure that they make an error they wouldn't otherwise make. For example, the receiver sees the server rushing toward the net and knows a floaty return will be an easy ball for the server to volley away — so they try to hit the ball lower or harder than they normally would, and end up hitting it into the net. Coming to net is about applying pressure, and sometimes that pressure alone is enough to win the point without ever hitting a volley.

Why Not Volley All the Time?

If the angles are so much better at the net, why don’t players just stand there? Because being at the net comes with serious risks — and getting there safely is no easy task either. Here are the big ones:

1. Less reaction time
You’re closer to your opponent, so the ball reaches you faster. A shot from your opponent may take a full second to reach the baseline, but only half a second to reach the net. That’s half the time to see the ball, decide what to do, and swing your racket.
2. Pace is harder to handle
A hard-hit ball is much harder to handle as a volley than if you let it bounce first. There’s a big drop in speed after the ball bounces, so groundstrokes from the baseline are easier to time. At the net, not only do you have less time (risk 1), you also have to absorb more speed at the moment of contact — a tough combination.
3. Vulnerable while moving forward
As you run towards the net, your opponent can aim their shot to bounce right at your feet. This is much harder for them to do if you’re at the baseline — a ball aimed at your feet would likely fly long and land out. But in the middle of the court, it’s a perfect target. Balls that land at your feet are extremely difficult to hit back, and you’re an easy target while you’re still getting into position.
4. Lobs go over your head
If your opponent sees you at the net, they can hit the ball high over you and make it land behind you. This is impossible to do when you’re at the baseline — there’s no court behind you for the ball to land in! But at the net, there’s a huge amount of open court behind you. Now you have to scramble backwards, which is one of the most awkward and slow movements in tennis.
5. Passing shots
Instead of lobbing, your opponent can hit a fast, low shot aimed to get past you down the sideline or cross-court. Commentators call this a “passing shot” — it’s one of the most exciting moments in tennis. Sometimes the ball flies clean past the net player and they can’t touch it. Other times the net player gets a racket on it but can’t control the volley because the shot was too low, too fast, or right at their feet. Either way, it’s still called a passing shot — the intent is to beat the net player, whether or not they touch it.

There's also a rule to be aware of: if you or your racket touch the net while the ball is in play, you lose the point immediately — even if you just made a brilliant volley. Players occasionally brush the net cord with their racket or get carried forward by their momentum. It's rare, but you'll see it on TV once in a while.

This is why players usually wait for the right moment to come to net — typically after hitting a shot that puts their opponent under pressure (hitting deep, hitting wide) or receiving a short ball they can attack. If they come in at the wrong time, their opponent will punish them.

How a Point Ends

Back in The Rally, we said that players take turns hitting the ball over the net and into the opponent's side of the court, and that each player must hit the ball back before it bounces a second time on their side. A point ends the moment someone fails to do that. There are only three ways it can happen:

In each animation below, the serve and return have already happened. Imagine this is where the players are after a few shots, and the point is about to end on Player A's next shot.

1. Ball hits the net
The ball doesn't make it over the net. The player who hit it loses the point. (This also covers the rare case where a shot is so weak it lands on your own side of the court without even reaching the net — for simplicity, we'll include those under "into the net" throughout this tutorial.)

2. Ball lands out
The ball goes over the net but lands outside the court — too long (past the baseline) or too wide (past the sideline). The player who hit it loses the point.

3. Ball bounces twice
The ball lands in the court, but the opponent can’t reach it before it bounces a second time. The player who hit the shot wins the point. This is what every player is trying to do — make the ball bounce twice before their opponent can get to it.

There are two common ways this happens, and they look very different:

The drop shot — a soft, delicate shot that barely clears the net and bounces very close to it, with so little energy that it barely moves after landing. The opponent can’t sprint forward fast enough to reach it. You’ll hear commentators say “oh, beautiful drop shot!” or “cheeky drop shot!” — it’s a finesse weapon.


The winner — a shot struck with such pace or placement that the opponent has no realistic chance of reaching it. The ball flies past them so fast that it would bounce a second time before they could get there. It may not even technically bounce twice — it might hit the fence behind the court first — but since the opponent had no chance, the point is over.

Drop shots and winners are opposites in style — one is soft and sneaky, the other is powerful and emphatic — but they’re both the same fundamental thing: the ball bounces twice before the opponent can reach it.

Rare but memorable: If the ball hits a player's body or clothing (rather than their racket), that player loses the point — even if the ball was clearly heading out. This is why you'll sometimes see a player at the baseline jump or twist sideways to dodge a powerful shot that's heading straight at them but sailing long — if they just stood there and let it hit them, they'd lose the point — even though the ball was clearly going out and was never going to land in the court. Occasionally you'll also see a fast shot (that may or may not have been going in) strike a player at the net before they can react — they lose the point, often accompanied by a wince and a rueful grin.

📺 What you'll hear on TV:
"Unforced error" — A player hits the ball into the net or out when they weren't under much pressure. It was their mistake, not a great shot by the opponent.

"Forced error" — A player hits the net or out, but only because their opponent's shot put them in a difficult position — maybe they had to sprint across the court, stretch for a wide ball, or deal with a shot hit with so much pace they couldn't control their return.

"Winner" — A shot so good the opponent couldn't touch it. Technically, a drop shot that bounces twice is also a winner — but on TV you'll usually hear "great drop shot!" for the soft kind, while "winner!" on its own almost always means the powerful kind that flies past the opponent.

Notice how these TV terms map directly to the three ways a point ends. An "unforced error" or "forced error" is always the ball hitting the net or landing out (ways 1 and 2) — the only difference is whether the player was under pressure. And a "winner" is always the ball bouncing twice (way 3).

In practice, the line between forced and unforced is a judgment call — commentators and statisticians don't always agree. The clear extremes are easy: a comfortable shot from the middle of the baseline hit into the net is clearly unforced, while sprinting at full stretch and barely reaching the ball is clearly forced. But many errors fall somewhere in between. Most errors in tennis are unforced (especially in recreational tennis), which is why commentators track them — they represent points given away rather than points earned by the opponent.

The Rally
Rally — the back-and-forth exchange of shots after the serve lands in.
Return — the receiver's first shot back. Must let the serve bounce once first.
Landing zone — during a rally, the ball can land anywhere on the opponent's half (not just the service box).
No lets in a rally — if the ball clips the net and lands in during a rally, play continues.
Three Phases of a Point
1. Serve — must land in the diagonal service box on the opposite side of the court.
2. Return — receiver must let the ball bounce once, then hit it back.
3. Rally — unrestricted. Either player can hit the ball before or after one bounce.
Shot Types
Forehand — any shot (groundstroke or volley) hit on your dominant hand side. Almost always one-handed.
Backhand — any shot hit on your non-dominant side. Can be one or two-handed.
Groundstroke — any shot hit after the ball bounces once. The most common shot in tennis.
Volley — hitting the ball before it bounces. Usually done near the net.
Lob — a high, arcing shot over an opponent who is at the net.
Passing shot — a shot aimed to beat an opponent at the net, whether it flies clean past them or forces a difficult volley they can't control.
Drop shot — a soft, delicate shot that barely clears the net and bounces so close to it that it quickly bounces a second time.
Shot Directions
Cross-court — hitting diagonally from one side to the other.
Down the line — hitting straight ahead, close to and parallel to the nearest sideline.
Down the middle — hitting toward the centre of the opponent's court, away from both sidelines.
Net Play
Why come forward? — two advantages: wider angles (the closer to the net, the sharper the angles you can hit) and taking time away from your opponent (the ball reaches them sooner, giving them less time to react).
Approach shot — the last shot a player hits before moving to the net. Usually off a short ball, or a great groundstroke that puts the opponent out of position.
Short ball — a shot that bounces shorter than usual (around the service line area), inviting the opponent forward.
Serve and volley — serving then immediately rushing to the net to volley the return.
How a Point Ends
Into the net — the ball hits the net and doesn't go over. Point to opponent.
Out — the ball bounces outside the court lines. Point to opponent.
Double bounce — the ball bounces twice on one side. That player loses the point.
What You'll Hear on TV
Winner — a shot so good the opponent can't reach it before it bounces twice (double bounce).
Unforced error — hitting into the net or out without pressure from the opponent.
Forced error — hitting into the net or out because the opponent's shot put you in difficulty.

📊 The Big Picture: Points, Games, Sets, Matches

Great work — you've learned how serves, rallies, and points work, and practised narrating real match footage. Now let's see how all those individual points add up to determine who wins.

Everything you've learned so far — serves, rallies, winners, errors — those are all individual points. Each time a point ends, one player wins it. But winning a single point doesn't win you the match. Tennis scoring works in layers, like boxes inside boxes:

The four layers of tennis scoring:

Points build into Games
Win enough points → you win a game

Games build into Sets
Win enough games → you win a set

Sets build into a Match
Win enough sets → you win the match

That's it. Every tennis match follows this structure, from your local park to the Wimbledon final.

We'll work through each layer one at a time, starting with the smallest: how points add up to win a game.

📊 The Rule: First to 4, Margin of 2

Before we get into how tennis displays scores, let's understand the actual rule. It's simpler than you'd think:

To win a game, you must:

1. Win at least 4 points, AND
2. Be ahead by at least 2 points

Both conditions must be true. Let's see what this looks like with plain numbers. (Throughout this section, we'll write Player A's score first — for example, 3-1 means Player A has won 3 points, Player B has won 1 point.)

When it's straightforward

Let's say Player A wins every point. After each point, we check: has anyone reached 4 points AND have a margin of 2?

Player A wins → 1-0. Reached 4? No. Game continues.
Player A wins → 2-0. Reached 4? No. Game continues.
Player A wins → 3-0. Reached 4? No. Game continues.
Player A wins → 4-0. Reached 4? Yes. Margin of 2? Yes (ahead by 4). ✓ Game over!

The game ended the moment both conditions were met for the first time. Now let's see a closer game — Player A wins 4 points but Player B wins 2 along the way:

Player A wins → 1-0. Game continues.
Player B wins → 1-1. Game continues.
Player A wins → 2-1. Game continues.
Player A wins → 3-1. Game continues.
Player B wins → 3-2. Game continues.
Player A wins → 4-2. Reached 4? Yes. Margin of 2? Yes. ✓ Game over!

When it gets interesting

Now imagine a tight game where the score reaches 3-2 and Player A wins the next point:

Player A wins → 4-2. Reached 4? Yes. Margin of 2? Yes. ✓ Game over!

But what if it was 3-2 and Player B wins instead?

Player B wins → 3-3. Reached 4? No. Game continues.

And from 3-3, if Player A wins:

Player A wins → 4-3. Reached 4? Yes. Margin of 2? No (only ahead by 1). ✗ Game continues!

This is the key moment — Player A has reached 4 points, but that's not enough on its own. The margin of 2 is missing. So the game must continue until someone pulls 2 clear.

From 4-3, there are two possibilities:

If Player A wins the next point:
Player A wins → 5-3. Reached 4? Yes. Margin of 2? Yes. ✓ Game over!

If Player B wins the next point instead:
Player B wins → 4-4. Margin of 2? No (tied). ✗ Game continues!

We're back to a tied score — effectively the same situation as 3-3. We'll explain this in detail below. And it can keep going: 5-5, 6-6, 7-7... The game only ends when someone finally pulls 2 clear.

A game might end in just 4 points (4-0), or it could take 20+ points if the players keep "trading" — that is, winning points alternately, so neither player can pull 2 ahead. That's what makes the margin-of-2 rule so exciting.

📊 The Key Insight: Only the Gap Matters

You've seen that a game can extend well beyond 4 points when players keep trading. Here's the crucial insight about why those extended games all work the same way:

The key insight: Once both players have won at least 2 points each, the actual numbers don't matter anymore — only the gap between them matters. Why? Because if both players have at least 2 points and one of them pulls ahead by 2, that player is guaranteed to have at least 4 points. The "at least 4 points" condition takes care of itself, so only the margin of 2 decides whether the game is over. Every score from that point falls into one of three situations, and each situation always plays out the same way regardless of the actual numbers. To see this, compare these pairs side by side:
3-2 is identical to 4-3 (Player A leads by 1):
If Player A wins the next point → 4-2 or 5-3. In both cases Player A has at least 4 points and leads by 2 — Player A wins the game.
If Player B wins the next point → 3-3 or 4-4. In both cases it's tied — neither player has a margin of 2, so the game continues.
2-3 is identical to 3-4 (Player B leads by 1):
If Player B wins the next point → 2-4 or 3-5. In both cases Player B has at least 4 points and leads by 2 — Player B wins the game.
If Player A wins the next point → 3-3 or 4-4. In both cases it's tied — neither player has a margin of 2, so the game continues.
2-2 is identical to 3-3 is identical to 4-4 (tied):
If Player A wins the next point → 3-2 or 4-3 or 5-4. In every case Player A leads by 1 — Player A is one point away from winning the game.
If Player B wins the next point → 2-3 or 3-4 or 4-5. In every case Player B leads by 1 — Player B is one point away from winning the game.

The pattern is always the same. Once both players have reached at least 2 points each, there are only ever three situations where the game is still ongoing: Player A leads by 1 (3-2, 4-3, 5-4, ...), Player B leads by 1 (2-3, 3-4, 4-5, ...), or it's tied (2-2, 3-3, 4-4, ...). As long as both players have at least 2 points each, what needs to happen from that moment for either player to win the game is exactly the same — no matter what the actual numbers are. The numbers just keep climbing until someone finally pulls 2 clear.

📊 Tennis's Quirky Labels

You now understand the rule: first to 4 points, margin of 2. Tennis follows this rule exactly — it just uses unusual names instead of plain numbers:

The translation:

0 points → "Love"
1 point → 15
2 points → 30
3 points → 40
4 points (with margin of 2) → Game won!

That's all there is to it. "Love" means zero, 15 means one point, 30 means two, 40 means three. The numbers are strange, but the rule underneath is the same one you just practised.

How the score is announced

The server's score is always said first. So if the server has won 2 points and the receiver has won 1 point, the score is announced as "30-15" (not "15-30").

When the score is tied, the word "all" is used:

Tied scores:

0-0 → "Love-all"
1-1 → "15-all"
2-2 → "30-all"
3-3 → This one gets its own name: "Deuce"

Deuce and Advantage

Remember what happens at 3-3? Neither player has won the game yet, and someone needs to pull ahead by 2. Tennis calls this situation deuce. But here's the important part: deuce doesn't just mean 3-3. It means any tied score from 3-3 onwards — 3-3, 4-4, 5-5, 12-12, and so on. You already know from The Key Insight that all these tied scores are the same situation, so they all get the same label: deuce.

From deuce, the next point gives one player the advantage — they're one point away from winning. Just as deuce covers every tied score from 3-3 up, advantage covers every "one ahead" score from that point on — 4-3, 5-4, 6-5 if the server leads, or 3-4, 4-5, 5-6 if the receiver leads. On TV, you'll hear:

"Ad-in" (or "advantage server") — the server won the point that was played at deuce, so they now lead by one (4-3, 5-4, 6-5, etc.)

"Ad-out" (or "advantage receiver") — the receiver won the point that was played at deuce, so they now lead by one (3-4, 4-5, 5-6, etc.)

Then:

If the player with advantage wins the next point → they win the game (margin of 2 achieved — exactly like going from 4-3 to 5-3, or 3-4 to 3-5, in plain numbers).

If the player with advantage loses the next point → the score goes back to deuce (the gap is gone — just like going from 4-3 back to 4-4, or 3-4 back to 4-4).

This can loop as many times as needed. Some games reach deuce five or six times before someone finally pulls ahead. The tension builds with every trip back to deuce, because both players have now won many points in this single game — and whichever player eventually loses the game has nothing to show for all that effort. That's because once a game is over, the points reset to 0-0 for the next game. Only the game itself counts — all those individual points vanish.

📺 What you'll hear on TV: The umpire calls out "deuce" or "advantage" followed by the player's name. Commentators say things like "back to deuce" or "advantage Djokovic." You might hear "this is the fifth deuce of this game!" — that means the players have been battling back and forth and neither can pull away.

📺 What you'll see on the scoreboard: TV scoreboards handle deuce and advantage differently from what the umpire says. At deuce, the scoreboard simply shows 40-40 — the umpire says "deuce" but the screen displays the numbers. At advantage, the scoreboard typically shows AD next to the player who has the advantage and blanks out the other player's point score. So you might see "AD" on Djokovic's row and nothing on Alcaraz's row — that means advantage Djokovic.

Putting it all together

Here are some example score announcements with both the tennis labels and the plain numbers you already know, so you can see the two side by side:

Example score announcements (server's score first):

Server has 30, receiver has 0 → "30-love" — in plain numbers: 2-0
Server has 0, receiver has 40 → "Love-40" — in plain numbers: 0-3
Server has 40, receiver has 15 → "40-15" — in plain numbers: 3-1
Server has 15, receiver has 30 → "15-30" — in plain numbers: 1-2
Both at 40 → "Deuce" — could be 3-3, 4-4, 5-5... we just don't know
Server wins the point at deuce → "Ad-in" — could be 4-3, 5-4, 6-5... we just don't know
Receiver wins the point at deuce → "Ad-out" — could be 3-4, 4-5, 5-6... we just don't know

Notice how the first four examples have specific plain numbers — 2-0, 0-3, 3-1, 1-2 — because at those scores the actual numbers still matter. But from deuce onwards, the labels deliberately hide the actual numbers, because (as you learned in The Key Insight) once both players have at least 2 points each, only the gap matters.

📊 Why the Labels Change at 40

Optional reading. This section explains the mathematical reasoning behind tennis's unusual labels. It's here for the curious — feel free to skip to the next section if you'd prefer to keep moving.

Now that you know the tennis labels, you might have noticed something odd. Back in The Key Insight, we showed that 2-2 and 3-3 are the same situation. But tennis calls 2-2 "30-all" and 3-3 "deuce" — two different labels for what is really the same situation. Why?

The answer is that when the scores are unequal and one player has fewer than 2 points, the actual numbers still matter. Compare these two situations:

3-1 (40-15): Player A leads by 2. If Player A wins the next point → 4-1. Player A has reached 4 points and leads by 3 — that's more than 2, so the margin condition is satisfied — game over.

2-0 (30-love): Player A also leads by 2. If Player A wins the next point → 3-0. Player A leads by 3, but has only reached 3 points — game is NOT over, because you need at least 4.

Same gap, completely different outcome — because the "at least 4 points" condition hasn't been met at 3-0 but has been met at 4-1. So tennis needs four distinct labels — 0, 1, 2, and 3 — because when one player hasn't yet reached 2 points, the specific numbers still matter (as 3-1 and 2-0 just showed). But a fifth label is unnecessary: once both players reach 2, every new score just repeats one of the three situations we already know (tied, one player leads by 1, or the other player leads by 1). There's nothing new to distinguish.

But once both players reach 3 points each (3-3), something changes. From that point on, whoever eventually pulls ahead by 2 will always have at least 4 points — guaranteed. So the "at least 4 points" condition takes care of itself, and only the gap matters. That's why tennis stops tracking the actual numbers at this point and switches to the special names: deuce (tied), advantage (leading by 1), and game (pulled ahead by 2).

Same situation, different labels

This creates a quirk. Remember the three equivalent pairs from The Key Insight? Now we can see that tennis gives each pair different names:

2-2 is called "30-all". 3-3 is called "deuce." We showed in The Key Insight that these are the same situation — tied, and the next point gives one player the lead (putting that player one point from winning the game) — but tennis uses different labels.

3-2 is called "40-30". 4-3 is called "ad-in." We showed these are the same situation too — if the server wins the next point, they win the game; if the receiver wins the next point, it's tied again — but different labels.

2-3 is called "30-40." 3-4 is called "ad-out." Again, the same situation — if the receiver wins the next point, they win the game; if the server wins the next point, it's tied again — but different labels.

They're the same situations in practice, just with different labels — because tennis needs "40" to exist as a distinct score for the cases where the opponent is still below 2 points (like "40-15" meaning 3-1, or "30-love" meaning 2-0, where the actual numbers genuinely matter and not just the differences).

You don't need to memorise any of this reasoning — it's here so you can see the logic behind tennis's unusual scoring.

📊 Holding and Breaking Serve

Who serves when?

Before a match begins, a coin toss decides who gets to choose first. The winner of the toss picks one thing: to serve, to receive, or which end of the court to start on. The loser then picks from whatever is left. For example: if the winner chooses to serve, the loser chooses which end they'd like to start on. Or if the winner chooses an end (perhaps to have the sun behind them), the loser chooses whether to serve or receive. From that point on, the players alternate serving after every game for the rest of the match. So if Player A serves game 1, Player B serves game 2, Player A serves game 3, and so on. One player serves the entire game — all the points within it — and then the other player takes over for the next game.

Within each game, the server alternates which side of the court they serve from, starting from the deuce side (right side) for the first point, then the ad side (left side) for the second point, then back to the deuce side, and so on. You may remember this from the Deuce and Ad Side section earlier in the tutorial.

Now that you know the scoring labels, here's a handy trick for TV viewers:

Which side is the server serving from? Take the plain-number equivalents of both players' scores and add them together. If the total is even → deuce side (right). If the total is odd → ad side (left).

For example: at 30-15 (that's 2 + 1 = 3, odd) → ad side. At 40-15 (3 + 1 = 4, even) → deuce side. And notice that at deuce (3 + 3 = 6, even), the serve is always from the deuce side — which is where the name comes from! After deuce, advantage is always from the ad side (3 + 4 = 7, odd), and if the score returns to deuce, it's back to the deuce side again.

The server's advantage

You already know from the Strategy section that serving is a big advantage — the server controls the point from the start, can aim for aces, and the receiver is always reacting. This advantage means the server is expected to win each game they serve.

In professional tennis, men hold serve roughly 80% of the time and women roughly 65%. The difference comes down to serve speed and height — men typically serve faster and their height lets them create sharper angles, especially out wide, making returns harder and giving them more free points on serve. Because of this advantage, tennis has specific terms for what happens:

Holding serve = the server wins the game they are serving. This is the expected outcome. If a commentator says "she holds," it means the server won her service game — business as usual.
Breaking serve = the receiver wins the game against the server. This is a significant event. If a commentator says "he breaks," it means the receiver overcame the server's advantage and won that game. Breaks of serve often decide who wins a set. Because men hold serve so much more often, a break in a men's match is an even bigger deal than in a women's match — though it's significant in both.

Why breaking serve matters so much

Think about it this way: if both players hold serve every time, the set score just keeps climbing evenly — 1-0, 1-1, 2-1, 2-2, 3-2, 3-3, and so on. Neither player pulls ahead. The only way to get ahead in a set is for one player to break their opponent's serve — winning a game where they weren't supposed to.

That's why you'll hear commentators get excited about break opportunities. A single break of serve can be the difference between winning and losing a set.

📺 What you'll hear on TV: "Djokovic holds to love" (won his service game without the opponent scoring a single point). "And she breaks! That could be the set right there!" (the receiver won the game — a big moment). "He's been broken twice in this set" (lost two of his service games — he's in trouble).

📊 Games Build Into Sets

Now let's zoom out. You know how points build into games. The next level up: games build into sets.

To win a set, you must:

1. Win at least 6 games, AND
2. Be ahead by at least 2 games

This should look familiar — it's the same "first to X, margin of 2" structure you learned for points within a game. So 6-4 wins a set (reached 6, ahead by 2). 6-3 wins. 6-2 wins. But 6-5 does not — that's only a margin of 1, so the set continues.

From 6-5, there are two possibilities:

If the leading player wins the next game → 7-5. They've reached at least 6 (because they have 7) and lead by 2 — set over.

If the trailing player wins the next game → 6-6. Tied — and something special happens (we'll cover this in The Tiebreak).

📊 Changeovers: Keeping Things Fair

Outdoor tennis courts aren't symmetrical in terms of conditions. One end might face the sun, making it harder to see the ball during the serve toss. One end might have the wind at your back, making your serves faster but your opponent's shots harder to control. The court surface might even wear differently on each side.

To make sure neither player gets an unfair advantage from these conditions, players switch ends of the court after every odd-numbered game — after the 1st game, after the 3rd, after the 5th, and so on.

Fair after just 2 games

Why does this work? Think about what happens after the first 2 games:

Games 1–2

Game 1: Player A serves from the north end. Player B receives from the south end. Players switch ends.
Game 2: Player B serves from the north end. Player A receives from the south end.

After just 2 games, both players have served from the same end (north) and both players have received from the same end (south). Sun, wind, and surface conditions have already been shared equally.

This matters because — as you've seen throughout tennis scoring — a margin of 2 is what determines progress. So even at this smallest meaningful scale, neither player has had a structural advantage.

A full rotation in 4 games

After another 2 games, the picture is even more complete:

Games 3–4

Game 3: Player A serves from the south end. Player B receives from the north end. Players switch ends.
Game 4: Player B serves from the south end. Player A receives from the north end.

Over all 4 games, both players have now served from both ends, and both players have received from both ends. That's a full rotation — and the pattern starts again from the beginning.

This cycle repeats throughout the entire set. No matter how conditions change during the match, both players face the same challenges for the same number of games. Tennis takes fairness seriously.

📺 What you'll hear on TV: "Players change ends" or "time for the changeover" — you'll see both players walk to their chairs on opposite sides for a 90-second break. This happens regularly throughout every match, and now you know why.

📊 The Tiebreak

When the set reaches 6-6, both players have won the same number of games and neither has a margin of 2. Rather than continuing indefinitely (which could take a very long time), tennis uses a special game called a tiebreak to decide the set.

How the tiebreak works

The tiebreak is simpler than a regular game in one way: it uses plain numbers — 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7... — not the quirky Love/15/30/40 labels.

To win a tiebreak, you must:

1. Win at least 7 points, AND
2. Be ahead by at least 2 points

The same "first to X, margin of 2" pattern again. So 7-5 wins the tiebreak, 7-4 wins, 7-3 wins. But 7-6 does not — the margin is only 1, so play continues until someone pulls 2 clear. Just like games within a game can go to deuce and back, tiebreaks can extend well beyond 7 points if the players keep trading points back and forth.

The winner of the tiebreak wins the set 7-6. The tiebreak score itself is shown alongside — you'll learn exactly how to read it in Sets Build Into a Match.

Serving in the tiebreak

You know from Holding and Breaking Serve that serving is a significant advantage. In a tiebreak, where every point matters, fairness in serving is crucial. The rule is: one player serves the first point, then each player serves 2 points at a time, alternating throughout.

Which player serves first? The player who received in the last regular game — it's simply the normal serve rotation continuing. Once the tiebreak is over, the serve passes back: the player who served first in the tiebreak becomes the receiver for the first game of the next set.

Why does the first server only get to serve for 1 point instead of 2? Because otherwise they'd have served 2 points before their opponent has served any — a 2-serve head start. By giving the first server only 1 serve, the serve counts stay as close together as possible (listed here as first server – second server): after 3 points, it's 1–2; after 5, it's 3–2; after 7, it's 3–4; after 9, it's 5–4. Notice how the 1-serve lead alternates between the two players — neither player ever gets more than 1 serve ahead, and both players take turns having that small advantage. This is the same fairness principle you saw with changing ends — tennis works hard to prevent either player from gaining an advantage from the structure of the game itself.

Mini-breaks

Serving still gives an advantage in the tiebreak, but it's less decisive than in a regular game. In a regular game, a male pro player might win around 60-65% of the individual points on their serve — but because they get multiple chances across 4 or more points, this compounds into winning a larger majority (roughly 80%) of their service games. In a tiebreak, each player only serves 1 or 2 points at a time, so that compounding effect is much smaller.

Winning a point on your opponent's serve during a tiebreak is called a mini-break. It's the tiebreak equivalent of breaking serve in a regular game, but on a smaller scale — and less significant. Because the serve advantage compounds less over just 1–2 points than over a full game, mini-breaks are easier to recover from. You're more likely to get a mini-break back than to recover from a full break of serve during a set. You'll hear commentators say things like "she's up a mini-break" meaning she has won one more point on her opponent's serve than her opponent has won on hers.

Why tiebreaks feel so dramatic

Remember how we described the tension at deuce — both players have invested effort winning points, but once the game is over, only the game itself counts and all those individual points vanish? The same thing happens at a larger scale with tiebreaks. Both players have won 6 games each in the set — they've invested enormous energy getting there — but after the tiebreak is over, only the set result matters. All those games disappear. The loser has nothing to show for them. That's why tiebreaks are often pivotal moments that can change the direction of a match, and why you'll feel the atmosphere shift on TV when a set reaches 6-6.

Switching ends in the tiebreak

The same fairness principle from Changeovers applies here too. Since a tiebreak can last many points, players switch ends after every 6 points. But unlike regular changeovers (where players sit down for 90 seconds), tiebreak changeovers are quick — players walk across and keep playing immediately. This is why tiebreaks feel so relentless on TV: there's no break in the tension.

Players also switch ends at the end of the tiebreak before starting the next set. (A tiebreak set ends at 7-6, which is 13 total games — an odd number — so the normal "switch after odd games" rule applies here too.)

How the score is announced

There's one more difference between tiebreaks and regular games. In a regular game, the server's score is always said first (as you learned in Tennis's Quirky Labels). In a tiebreak, this changes: the higher score is said first, followed by the name of the player who is leading. If the score is tied, it's called "all" — for example, "3-all."

Regular game: "30-15" — the server has 30, the receiver has 15 (server's score always first).

Tiebreak: "4-3 Djokovic" — Djokovic is leading 4 to 3 (leading player's score always first, regardless of who is serving). "5-all" — the score is tied at 5 each. "1-zero Nadal" — Nadal leads 1 to 0.

Note: Tiebreaks use "zero" instead of "love" — you'll hear "1-zero" rather than "1-love."
📺 What you'll hear on TV: "We're headed to a tiebreak" — the tension rises noticeably. The umpire calls out the score with the leading player's name: "4-3 Djokovic," "5-all," "6-5 Alcaraz." "She gets the mini-break!" (won a point on the opponent's serve). If someone wins from a long tiebreak, you might hear "she takes the tiebreak 10-8 to win the set 7-6."

📊 Sets Build Into a Match

You now know how points build into games, and games build into sets. The final level: sets build into a match.

Best of 3: First player to win 2 sets wins the match.

Best of 5: First player to win 3 sets wins the match.

Most professional tennis uses best of 3 sets — this includes all women's matches and most men's tournaments. Best of 5 sets is reserved for men's matches at the four Grand Slams (the Australian Open, French Open, Wimbledon, and the US Open — the four biggest tournaments in tennis).

No margin of 2 here

This is an important difference from everything else you've learned. Points within a game need a margin of 2. Games within a set need a margin of 2. Points within a tiebreak need a margin of 2. But sets within a match do NOT. In a best-of-3 match, winning 2 sets to 1 is enough — you don't need to be ahead by 2 sets. In a best-of-5 match, winning 3 sets to 2 is enough.

Reading a match score

When a match result is written out — on websites, in news headlines, or on commentary graphics — there's one important convention: the match winner's score in each set is always listed first, even in sets they lost. This makes it easy to read at a glance: every number on the left belongs to the winner. Let's walk through one:

A worked example

Player A defeated Player B: 6-4, 6-7 (3-7), 6-2

Let's break this down set by set:

6-4 — Player A won the first set 6 games to 4.

6-7 (3-7) — Player A lost the second set. The games went to 6-6, triggering a tiebreak, which Player A lost 3-7. The tiebreak score is shown in brackets. Even though Player A lost this set, their score is still written first (6-7, not 7-6) — that's the convention.

6-2 — Player A won the deciding third set 6 games to 2.

On TV scoreboards that show each player on a separate row, the same tiebreak detail appears as small superscript numbers next to each player's set score:

Player A   6   63   6
Player B   4   77   2

Both formats tell you the same thing — just presented differently depending on where you're reading it.

More examples

Now that you've seen how to read one, here are a few more to build your confidence:

6-4, 6-3 — A best-of-3 match won in 2 sets (called "straight sets" — the winner never lost a set). The winner took the first set 6-4 and the second set 6-3.

6-4, 3-6, 7-6 (7-5) — A best-of-3 match that went to 3 sets. The winner took the first set 6-4, lost the second 3-6 (their score is still written first, even though they lost this set), then won the third-set tiebreak 7 points to 5. A close, hard-fought match.

6-3, 6-7 (4-7), 6-2 — The winner took the first set 6-3, lost the second-set tiebreak 4 points to 7, then won the deciding third set 6-2.

6-4, 4-6, 6-4, 3-6, 7-5 — A best-of-5 match that went the full distance — 5 sets. The final set was won 7-5 — no tiebreak needed, because the winner pulled ahead at 6-5 and won the next game. These marathon matches can last 4 or 5 hours and are among the most dramatic events in sport.
📺 What you'll hear on TV: "Straight sets" means the winner won every set — a dominant performance. "It went to a deciding set" or "a fifth set" means the match was close. "He won in five" is shorthand for a best-of-5 match that needed all 5 sets.

📊 Reading a TV Scoreboard

Now that you understand the full scoring system, let's put it into practice. When you watch tennis on TV, you'll see a scoreboard like this in the corner of the screen:

🎾 C. Alcaraz 0 3 15
🎾 N. Djokovic 1 4 30
🎾 Sets Games Points

Let's break down what each part means:

🎾 (tennis ball icon) — appears next to the player who is currently serving. In this example, Djokovic is serving (bottom row).

Sets column (leftmost numbers) — how many sets each player has won so far. Djokovic has won 1 set, Alcaraz has won 0 sets.

Games column (middle numbers) — how many games each player has won in the current set. Djokovic has won 4 games, Alcaraz has won 3.

Points column (rightmost numbers) — the score in the current game. Each player's points appear on their own row: Alcaraz has 15, Djokovic has 30. The umpire would announce this as "30-15" (server's score first), but on the scoreboard you simply read each player's score from their row — the server can be on either row.

Putting it all together: Djokovic is serving, leads the match 1 set to 0, leads the current set 4 games to 3, and leads the current game 30-15.

But you can read even more from this scoreboard using what you've learned. Djokovic is serving, which means the players have been alternating serves and it's currently Djokovic's turn. Since Djokovic is serving the 8th game (4 + 3 = 7 completed), that means Alcaraz served first in this set — serving games 1, 3, 5, and 7 (four games), while Djokovic served games 2, 4, and 6 (three completed games).

If both players had held serve every time, Alcaraz would lead 4-3. But Djokovic leads 4-3 instead — so Djokovic has broken Alcaraz's serve one more time than Alcaraz has broken his.

At one extreme, that could be a single clean break with no breaks back — comfortably the most likely scenario, given that professional players hold serve the vast majority of the time. At the other extreme, it could theoretically mean Djokovic broke Alcaraz 4 times and Alcaraz broke back 3 — a wild, chaotic set with 7 breaks of serve in 7 games. Either way, Djokovic is up a net break — a significant advantage.

More examples

🎾 I. Swiatek 1 6 5
🎾 A. Sabalenka 1 6 4

This scoreboard shows a tiebreak in progress. Both players have won 1 set each and 6 games each in the current set — so they're in a tiebreak. Sabalenka is serving. The tiebreak score is 5-4 — notice the points are plain numbers, not Love/15/30/40. The umpire would announce this as "5-4 Swiatek" (leading score first, then the leading player's name — remember, tiebreaks use a different convention from regular games where the server's score is said first).

If Sabalenka doesn't score again, Swiatek needs at least 2 more points to win the tiebreak (reaching 7 with a margin of 2) and take the set. If Swiatek doesn't score again, Sabalenka needs at least 3 more points (reaching 7 with a margin of 2, currently only on 4). Of course, if both keep winning points on their serve, the tiebreak will extend beyond 7.

In fact, that's exactly the story so far: at 5-4, there are no net mini-breaks — Swiatek leads only because she served first and has had one more serve point. It's possible neither player has mini-broken the other at all, or there may have been mini-breaks in both directions that cancelled out.

🎾 R. Nadal 2 5 40
🎾 R. Federer 2 4 30

This is a dramatic moment. It's a best-of-5 match (Grand Slam), tied at 2 sets each — so this is the deciding fifth set. Nadal is serving, leading 5-4 in games, and leading 40-30 in the current game.

Let's apply the same reasoning as above. Nadal is serving the 10th game (5 + 4 = 9 completed), so Federer served first — serving games 1, 3, 5, 7, and 9 (five games), while Nadal served games 2, 4, 6, and 8 (four completed games). If both had held every serve, Federer would lead 5-4. But Nadal leads 5-4 instead — so Nadal has broken Federer's serve one more time than Federer has broken his. Nadal is up a net break and is now serving to win the match.

If Nadal wins this next point, he wins this game (making it 6-4 in games), which wins the set, which wins the match. That single point is simultaneously a game point (he's the server, one point from winning the game), a set point (winning this game wins the set), and a match point (winning this set wins the match). You'll learn these terms properly in the next section — but you can already see why the commentators would be on the edge of their seats.

📊 Key Moments: Game Point, Break Point, Set Point, Match Point

Tennis commentators use specific terms for the moments when a player is one point away from winning something. Understanding these terms will help you follow the drama as it unfolds.

Game point — the server is one point away from winning the current game. This is the expected outcome (servers hold most of the time), so game point is relatively routine.
Break point — the receiver is one point away from winning the game against the server. This is a much more significant moment than game point, because as we discussed in Holding and Breaking Serve, the server is expected to win their service game. Saving a break point (the server wins the point despite being one point from losing the game) is a big deal — but converting a break point (the receiver wins it) is an even bigger deal, because it can change the course of a set.

What scores create break points? Any score where the receiver has 40 and the server has less than 40, or when the score is ad-out (advantage receiver). In each case, if the receiver wins the very next point, they win the game and break serve:

30-40 — one break point. The receiver wins the next point → they break serve. If the server wins instead → deuce.

15-40 — two break points. The receiver has two chances to break: if they win the next point → they break serve immediately. Even if the server wins the next point (making it 30-40), the receiver still has another break point.

Love-40 — three break points (called "triple break point"). The receiver has three chances to convert before the server can get back to deuce.

Ad-out — one break point. The receiver won the point at deuce and now needs just one more to break serve. If the server wins instead → back to deuce.

Notice these are two different terms for two different situations — game point is for the server, break point is for the receiver. You won't hear commentators say "game point" when the receiver is about to win — they'll always call it "break point."

Set point — a player is one point away from winning the current set. In a regular game, a set point is always also either a game point (if the server has it) or a break point (if the receiver has it) — because winning the set means winning the game first. The exception is during a tiebreak, where set point is just set point — the game point / break point distinction doesn't apply because tiebreaks don't use those terms.
Match point — a player is one point away from winning the entire match. A match point is also always a set point — because winning the match means winning a set. In a regular game, it's also either a game point or a break point depending on who is serving — but during a tiebreak, it's simply match point and set point. This is the most dramatic moment in tennis.
Championship point — a match point at a Grand Slam final or other major tournament final. It's the same thing as match point, but commentators use "championship point" to mark the magnitude of the occasion — one point from lifting the trophy.

Multiple labels at once

A single point can carry multiple labels simultaneously. Look at these two scoreboards — they show the exact same game score, but with one difference: who's serving.

Scenario 1: Nadal serving
🎾 R. Nadal 2 5 40
🎾 R. Federer 2 4 30
Scenario 2: Federer serving
🎾 R. Nadal 2 5 40
🎾 R. Federer 2 4 30

The numbers are identical: Nadal has 3 points to Federer's 2 in the current game, leads 5-4 in games, and it's 2 sets each — the deciding set. But look where the 🎾 is — that one detail changes everything:

Scenario 1 (Nadal serving):
✓ Game point — Nadal (the server) is one point from winning the game
✓ Set point — winning the game would give Nadal the set 6-4
✓ Match point — winning the set would give Nadal the match 3-2

It's a game point (not a break point) because Nadal is the server — the expected outcome is that he wins.
Scenario 2 (Federer serving):
✓ Break point — Nadal (the receiver) is one point from winning a game on Federer's serve
✓ Set point — winning the game would give Nadal the set 6-4
✓ Match point — winning the set would give Nadal the match 3-2

Now it's a break point — Nadal is the receiver about to win on Federer's serve. The score would be announced as 30-40 (Federer's score first, since he's serving). Commentators would go wild: "Nadal has match point on the Federer serve!"

In practice, when a point carries multiple labels, commentators will typically use only the highest-level one. The hierarchy from top to bottom is: match point → set point → break point / game point. So even though Nadal's point in Scenario 1 is simultaneously a game point, a set point, and a match point, you'll hear the commentator simply say "match point" — because that's the most significant thing at stake.

📺 What you'll hear on TV: "Game point" — the server is about to close out the game (routine). "Break point!" — the receiver is threatening to win the game (tense). "Set point for Djokovic" — one point from taking the set. "Championship point!" — one point from winning the title at a Grand Slam final. "Match point!" — one point from winning the match. "He saves three match points!" — a player was one point from losing the match three separate times and won all three of those points. That's incredibly clutch — a term you'll hear for players who perform their best under the most intense pressure, delivering when it matters most.
